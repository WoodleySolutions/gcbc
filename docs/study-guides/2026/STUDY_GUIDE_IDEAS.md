# 2026 Study Guide Ideas & Narrative Parallels

This document captures ideas for narrative pedagogy in the 2026 study guides - specific stories, historical figures, or parallel texts that can be used to demonstrate theological truths through concrete examples.

## Starship Troopers (February)

**Theme:** Authority and Duty

### Philosophical Framework: Natural Law, Aristotle, and Divine Revelation

**The Core Argument:**
Heinlein's Federation has a sophisticated moral philosophy. They get virtue formation right - duty, discipline, courage, self-sacrifice. They understand that these things make you a better human being. They've arrived at natural law through reason alone.

This is the Aristotelian achievement: understanding virtue, human flourishing, and the good life without divine revelation. And it's genuinely impressive. Aquinas said Aristotle got as close as a human being could get to truth through reason alone.

But there's a ceiling. Without divine revelation, you can't know the *ultimate* purpose of virtue. You can't know that human beings are made for communion with God. You can't know that the virtues you develop are training for eternal life.

**The Parallel: Aristotle → Aquinas / Federation → Church**

Just as Aquinas completed Aristotle by adding divine revelation to natural philosophy, the Church completes what the Federation begins.

**Narrative Structure:**
1. **Show the Federation's Philosophy**: Rico's formation, the virtues it develops, the moral reasoning behind it (textual evidence from Dubois's lectures)
2. **Enter Aristotle**: What the Greeks discovered about virtue, human flourishing, the good life - and how far reason can take you
3. **Aquinas's Achievement**: "Grace perfects nature" - divine revelation doesn't contradict natural law, it completes it
4. **The Parallel with Ignatius**: Both Rico and Ignatius receive the same natural formation. Both develop genuine virtues. But Ignatius receives divine revelation that shows him what it's all for
5. **The Fullness**: What the Church teaches that the Federation (and Aristotle) can't know

**Key Philosophical Concepts:**
- **Natural Law**: Moral truths accessible through reason (don't murder, honor your parents, seek the good)
- **Virtue Ethics**: You become virtuous by practicing virtue (Aristotle's *Nicomachean Ethics*)
- **Teleology**: Everything has a purpose/end (telos) - but what's the ultimate telos of human life?
- **Grace Perfects Nature**: Divine revelation doesn't replace natural law, it elevates it

**Aquinas on Aristotle:**
- Called him simply "The Philosopher"
- Believed Aristotle had gotten as far as human reason could go
- Showed how Christian revelation completes (not contradicts) Aristotelian philosophy
- "Grace does not destroy nature but perfects it"

**The Federation's Ceiling:**
- They know duty forms character
- They know virtue makes you more fully human
- They know self-sacrifice is noble
- But they can only point to survival and citizenship as the ultimate purpose
- They have natural law but not divine law

**What Divine Revelation Adds:**
- Human beings are made in God's image (imago Dei)
- We're created for eternal communion with the Trinity
- Every virtue we develop is training for the beatific vision
- Love (caritas) is the highest virtue and our ultimate purpose
- We have dignity not because we're useful but because we're beloved by God

**Rico vs. Ignatius:**
- Both receive the same natural formation (military discipline, virtue development)
- Both become genuinely better men (courage, loyalty, self-sacrifice)
- But Ignatius receives divine revelation that shows him the *telos*
- Rico has natural virtue; Ignatius has natural virtue elevated by supernatural grace
- The Federation teaches Rico how to be a good soldier; the Church teaches Ignatius how to be a saint

**Scripture and Magisterium:**
- Romans 1:20 - "What can be known about God is plain to them, because God has shown it to them. For his invisible attributes... have been clearly perceived in the things that have been made."
- Romans 2:14-15 - "When Gentiles, who do not have the law, do by nature what the law requires... they show that the work of the law is written on their hearts."
- CCC 1954-1960 on Natural Law
- CCC 1803-1845 on the Virtues

**Contemporary Application:**
- Why secular virtue ethics gets so much right but ultimately falls short
- The modern world's attempt to have morality without God (and why it's unstable)
- How to talk to non-believers about virtue in a way they can understand (start with natural law)
- Why "be a good person" isn't enough without knowing what "good" is *for*

**Potential Narrative Flow (Revised Opening):**
Instead of opening with Rico and Ignatius side-by-side as soldiers, we could open with:

1. **The Federation's Moral Philosophy** (show through Dubois's lectures, Rico's formation)
2. **Aristotle's Achievement** (how far can reason take you?)
3. **Enter Ignatius** (a man who received the same formation Rico did, plus something more)
4. **The Fullness** (what divine revelation adds to natural law)

This keeps the narrative pedagogy but situates it within a clearer philosophical framework - showing rather than telling how natural law needs divine revelation to reach its completion.

## Master and Commander (August)

**Theme:** Honor in Command

### Narrative Parallel: David and Bathsheba

**The Parallel:**
Captain Jack Aubrey loses the prize of the Cacafuego - the greatest capture of his career, the fortune that would secure his crew's future - because he chooses to dally with the admiral's wife instead of staying on mission and acting with virtue.

King David, at the height of his power and success, sees Bathsheba bathing. Instead of turning away, he sends for her. This single act of self-indulgence leads to adultery, murder (Uriah), and consequences that haunt his reign forever.

**Key Connections:**
- Both are great leaders at the peak of their success
- Both make a choice to pursue forbidden pleasure instead of doing their duty
- Both lose something irreplaceable as a consequence
- Both face the reality that authority doesn't exempt you from moral law
- Both stories ask: What good is external success if you lose your integrity?

**Theological Framework:**
- Servant leadership vs. abuse of power
- The cost of vice (even "small" indulgences)
- Authority as responsibility, not privilege
- How one moment of weakness can undo years of virtue
- Nathan's confrontation with David: "You are the man" (2 Samuel 12:7)

**Narrative Structure:**
1. Show Aubrey's choice and its consequences
2. Tell David's story in parallel
3. Explore what the Church teaches about authority, virtue, and the abuse of power
4. Contemporary application: How do we guard against using our positions (boss, parent, etc.) to justify self-indulgence?

**Scripture References:**
- 2 Samuel 11-12 (David and Bathsheba, Nathan's confrontation)
- Psalm 51 (David's repentance: "Create in me a clean heart, O God")
- Luke 12:48 ("To whom much is given, much will be required")

## Hyperion (October)

**Theme:** Suffering and Redemption

### Historical Context: John Keats and the Hyperion Poems

**The Connection:**
Dan Simmons's *Hyperion* is deeply indebted to the Romantic poet John Keats. The title comes directly from Keats's unfinished epic poems *Hyperion* and *The Fall of Hyperion*, and the character of the poet in Simmons's novel is literally named Martin Silenus after a figure from Keats's life.

**Why This Matters for Readers:**
Many readers struggle with *Hyperion* because they don't recognize the Romantic/Keatsian framework Simmons is working within. Understanding Keats helps unlock the novel's deeper themes about:
- Beauty and suffering
- The role of the artist/poet in bearing witness to pain
- Mortality and immortality
- The sublime and the grotesque

**John Keats Background:**
- Born 1795, died 1821 at age 25 of tuberculosis
- Watched his mother and brother die of the same disease
- Fell in love with Fanny Brawne but was too poor and sick to marry her
- Wrote his greatest poetry knowing he was dying
- Famous for "negative capability" - the ability to dwell in mystery and doubt without reaching for certainty
- His *Hyperion* poems explore the fall of the Titans and the rise of the Olympians - old gods replaced by new

**Key Keats Poems to Reference:**
- *Hyperion* and *The Fall of Hyperion* (obviously)
- "Ode on a Grecian Urn" ("Beauty is truth, truth beauty")
- "When I Have Fears That I May Cease to Be" (mortality and artistic legacy)
- "Bright Star" (love and permanence)

**Theological Bridge:**
- Keats wrote about beauty and suffering without Christian faith (he was skeptical/agnostic)
- But his intuition that beauty and truth are connected is deeply Catholic
- His wrestling with mortality points toward the Resurrection even if he couldn't name it
- The poet as witness to suffering → Christ as the ultimate witness
- Keats's early death and unfinished work → the Christian understanding of suffering as redemptive, not meaningless

**Narrative Structure:**
1. Introduce Keats's life and tragic early death
2. Explain the *Hyperion* poems and what Simmons is doing with them
3. Show how Keats's themes (beauty/suffering/mortality) run through Simmons's novel
4. Explore what the Church teaches about redemptive suffering that Keats couldn't fully articulate
5. The poet's vocation to bear witness → our vocation to bear witness to Christ

**Contemporary Application:**
- How do we find meaning in unfinished lives? (miscarriage, early death, unfulfilled dreams)
- The artist's calling to witness truth even in suffering
- Beauty as a bridge to God (Keats almost got there)

## Notes for Future Development

These narrative parallels follow the "show, don't tell" pedagogy established in the Starship Troopers essay (Rico/Ignatius). The goal is to let the stories teach the theology rather than lecturing about abstract principles.

Both of these will need:
- Careful research to get details right
- Specific scenes from the novels to anchor the parallels
- Clear theological articulation without being preachy
- Discussion questions that invite readers into the parallel
