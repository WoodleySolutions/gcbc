## I. Core Principles

### Authenticity Over Performance
We are not writing to prove our Catholic credentials or perform for a traditional audience. We write for readers who want to dig in with intellectual rigor and genuine reverence. We ask hard questions. We wrestle with uncomfortable truths. We trust our readers to think deeply alongside us.

**What This Means:**
- Don't shy away from difficult theological questions
- Challenge traditional interpretations when warranted
- Acknowledge complexity and ambiguity where it exists
- Invite dialogue rather than prescribe answers
- Focus on substance over form ("the Catholic costume")

### Intellectual Rigor
We treat our readers as serious thinkers capable of engaging with complex moral, theological, and literary analysis. We don't dumb down. We don't hand-wave. We do the work.

**What This Means:**
- Deep textual engagement with the books
- Sophisticated theological reasoning
- Careful use of Catechism, Scripture, and Church teaching
- Historical and cultural context when relevant
- Logical argumentation, not emotional manipulation

### The "War is Heck" Lens
Every book on the 2026 list explores conflict—physical, moral, spiritual. Our unifying question: **What does it look like to do the right thing when it might cost your own life? What does it look like to choose virtue when cruelty and vice are all around you?**

**Key Themes to Explore:**
- Virtue in extremis (courage, prudence, justice, temperance under fire)
- Just War theory and its applications/limitations
- Authority, duty, and obedience
- Brotherhood and loyalty
- Martyrdom and sacrifice
- Spiritual warfare
- Mercy toward enemies and "the other"
- The relationship between temporal and eternal conflict

### Dual Voices, Unified Methodology
Tyler and Sadie each bring distinct writing styles while sharing a common approach. The study guide will be attributed to "Tyler & Sadie Woodley" without individual bylines for each essay.

**Sadie's Voice:**
- Essay-driven, rhetorically sophisticated
- Moves from specific to universal
- Uses extended metaphors and comparative analysis
- Deeply personal yet broadly applicable
- Strong narrative arc within each essay

**Tyler's Voice:**
- Informational/educational focus
- Warm and entertaining, not dry or academic
- Clear explanations of complex ideas
- Personal reflection in service of teaching
- Direct engagement with hard questions

**Both Voices Share:**
- Conversational yet rigorous
- Use of "we/us" to create intimacy with reader
- Humor where appropriate (never forced)
- Rhetorical questions to engage reader
- Modern references and applications
- Care for reader's intellectual and spiritual journey

## II. Thematic Framework: "War is Heck"

### The Narrative Arc of 2026
Our reading list traces a powerful journey through the landscape of human conflict:

1. **Opens with martyrdom** - Faithfulness unto death (*The Power and the Glory*)
2. **Builds through duty** - Military, civic, and personal obligation (*Starship Troopers*, *Leviathan Wakes*, *Master and Commander*)
3. **Explores fellowship** - Loyalty and brotherhood in battle (*The Three Musketeers*, LOTR trilogy)
4. **Reveals spiritual dimensions** - The unseen war against evil (*The Screwtape Letters*)
5. **Culminates in sacrificial victory** - Grace breaking into despair (*Hyperion*, *The Return of the King*)

### Finding Catholic Truth in Stories of War

**Not All War is Just:**
We acknowledge that war, even "just war," is a tragic consequence of the fall. War is not glorious in itself—it is "heck" (a deliberate understatement to honor the complexity). Our task is to find virtue, grace, and truth even in the midst of violence and conflict.

**The Spiritual Battle:**
All temporal conflict points to the greater spiritual warfare described by St. Paul in Ephesians 6:12: "For our struggle is not against flesh and blood, but against the rulers, against the authorities, against the powers of this dark world and against the spiritual forces of evil in the heavenly realms." Physical battles in these stories illuminate the unseen war for souls.

**Virtue Under Pressure:**
War reveals character. In moments of crisis, the masks come off. We see who people really are. The battlefield—literal or metaphorical—is where virtue is tested and refined.

**Sacrifice and Redemption:**
The ultimate "war" story is the Passion of Christ—God entering into conflict with sin, death, and evil, and conquering through self-sacrifice. Every story of sacrificial love in our 2026 list echoes this eternal truth.

### Key Theological Touchpoints

**Just War Theory:**
Developed by Augustine and Aquinas, the Church's teaching on just war provides criteria for evaluating military conflict:
- Just cause
- Legitimate authority
- Right intention
- Probability of success
- Proportionality
- Last resort
- Comparative justice

Use these criteria to analyze conflicts in the books. Don't be afraid to conclude that a war in a story fails to meet just war standards—that's part of the analysis.

**The Cardinal Virtues in Combat:**
- **Prudence**: Right reason in action, especially in life-or-death decisions
- **Justice**: Giving each person their due, even enemies
- **Fortitude**: Courage in the face of mortal danger
- **Temperance**: Self-control in the midst of chaos and violence

**The Theological Virtues:**
- **Faith**: Trust in God when circumstances are dire
- **Hope**: Confidence in ultimate victory even when temporal defeat looms
- **Charity**: Love of enemy, willingness to sacrifice for others

**Martyrdom:**
The Greek word *martyria* means "witness." A martyr witnesses to the truth by dying for it. Martyrdom is the highest form of Christian witness and the ultimate victory in spiritual warfare. Explore what makes a true martyr vs. a mere casualty.

## III. Structural Requirements

### Required Elements (In Order)

1. **Title**
   - Creative, thematic, engaging
   - Should hint at the central question/theme of the essay
   - Examples from 2025: "What is Childhood Innocence, anyway?", "The End. What a Beautiful Beginning!", "Love, Actually… is Complicated"

2. **Author Attribution**
   - Format: `**By [Author Name]**`
   - Example: `**By Graham Greene**`

3. **Horizontal Rule**
   - Use `---` to create visual separation

4. **Opening Quote**
   - Source: Catechism of the Catholic Church, Scripture, or writings of the Saints
   - **Fit over formula**: Choose the source that best illuminates your central argument
   - Format examples:
     - CCC: `— *Catechism of the Catholic Church* (para. 1234)`
     - Scripture: `— John 15:13`
     - Saints: `— St. Augustine, *The City of God*`
   - The quote should be substantial (2-5 sentences ideal)
   - Should directly relate to your central thesis
   - Can use multiple quotes if they work together

5. **Body Essay**
   - Length: 2000-4000 words
   - See Section IV for style guidelines

6. **Discussion Questions**
   - Exactly **5 questions**
   - Format: Numbered list (1-5)
   - See Section V for crafting guidelines

### Optional Elements

**Subheadings:**
Use markdown `##` subheadings to break up long essays and guide the reader through your argument. Subheadings should be:
- Thematic, not generic ("The Superpower of Free Will" not "Analysis")
- Witty or provocative when appropriate
- Clear signposts for the reader

**Block Quotes:**
Use `>` for extended quotations from the book being analyzed. Keep these focused and purposeful—quote to illuminate, not to pad length.

**Examples from 2025:**
Look to the 2025 study guides for structural models:
- *Ender's Game*: Multiple subheadings organizing complex theological argument
- *Pride and Prejudice*: Each bad marriage example gets its own section
- *Dune*: Theological concept + application structure

## IV. Writing Style Guidelines

### PRIMARY PEDAGOGY: Narrative First

**Show, Don't Tell:**
Our primary teaching method is narrative. We tell stories. We show transformation, virtue, conflict, and grace through concrete examples - from the books, from the lives of the saints, from Scripture. Rather than explaining theological concepts abstractly, we demonstrate them through the lived experience of characters and real people.

**Structure of Narrative Pedagogy:**
1. **Tell the Story**: Present a narrative (from the book, from a saint's life, from Scripture) that embodies the truth you're exploring
2. **Show the Truth**: Let the narrative itself reveal the theological or moral principle
3. **Deepen Understanding**: Once shown, you can articulate the principle clearly - but briefly
4. **Apply**: Connect to contemporary life through more narrative (reader's potential experience)

**Example:**
- **NOT THIS**: "The Church teaches that duty forms character. Through the practice of virtue, we become virtuous."
- **BUT THIS**: "Rico enlisted to follow a girl. The Mobile Infantry made him run until his lungs burned, obey without question, put his squad before himself. Day after day, drill after drill. And somewhere in that crucible, the spoiled rich kid became someone who would die for his brothers. He didn't decide to be noble. Duty shaped him into someone capable of nobility."

**Why Narrative Works:**
- Engages the imagination, not just the intellect
- Makes abstract truths concrete
- Allows readers to discover truth alongside you
- Mirrors how Scripture teaches (parables, history, poetry)
- Creates emotional investment, which aids retention
- Respects the reader's intelligence (they can see the truth themselves)

**When to Use Narrative:**
- Opening hooks (always)
- Demonstrating transformation/virtue in action
- Showing theological concepts at work
- Making contemporary applications relatable
- Building to your central thesis

**When Direct Teaching is Appropriate:**
- Clear definitions of terms (brief, then move to narrative)
- Citing Church teaching for authority
- Making precise moral distinctions
- Correcting common misunderstandings
- Brief transitions between narrative sections

**Parallel Lives:**
One powerful narrative technique: run parallel stories (e.g., Rico and St. Ignatius of Loyola). Both experience similar formation through duty, but one discovers the supernatural dimension while the other doesn't. The comparison itself teaches without heavy-handed explanation.

### Tone & Voice

**Conversational Yet Rigorous:**
Write as if you're having an intense, fascinating conversation with a smart friend over coffee. You're both leaning in, engaged, challenging each other. This isn't a formal academic paper, but it's not casual fluff either.

**Good:**
> "Well, St. John Chrysostom, that sounds beautiful, but what if husband and wife are of the same mind in being equally terrible?"

**Avoid:**
> "One might inquire as to the applicability of Chrysostom's maxim in cases where both spouses demonstrate moral deficiency."

**Also Avoid:**
> "LOL, Chrysostom didn't think about two terrible people getting married, did he?"

**Use "We" and "Us":**
Include the reader in your exploration. You're on this journey together.
- "We see in this character..."
- "How do we reconcile..."
- "This challenges us to consider..."

**Occasional "You":**
When directly challenging or inviting the reader:
- "You cannot unlearn what you know."
- "What would you do in this situation?"

**Humor:**
Use humor purposefully, not gratuitously. It should:
- Illuminate a truth
- Create relief in heavy material
- Build rapport with reader
- Never undercut the seriousness of the subject

**Examples of Good Humor:**
- "DON'T PANIC" (from 2025 Introduction, riffing on *Hitchhiker's Guide*)
- "Eww" (as a subheading for Charlotte/Mr. Collins marriage analysis)
- Calling the Kwisatz Haderach the "super-man" and "Kool-Aid"

### When to Be Formal vs. Informal

**More Formal:**
- Explaining complex theological concepts
- Citing Church teaching
- Making careful moral distinctions
- Presenting historical context

**More Conversational:**
- Opening hooks
- Transitions between ideas
- Contemporary applications
- Rhetorical questions
- Conclusions that invite reader response

**The Shift:**
Many essays will move from conversational opening to more formal analysis, then back to conversational conclusion. This rhythm keeps readers engaged while doing serious intellectual work.

### Handling Difficult Theological Questions

**Don't Hedge:**
If you're going to ask a hard question, really ask it. Don't soften it so much that it loses its edge.

**Good:**
> "God could be accused of using child soldiers. There is the case of young King David... These are only a few examples of the children that suffered horrific, painful childhoods and deaths for the Lord."

**Weak:**
> "Some people wonder whether God's use of young people in difficult situations might seem harsh to modern sensibilities."

**Answer with Depth:**
After posing a difficult question, do the work of answering it. Don't leave readers hanging with "mystery" as a cop-out. Mystery is real, but lazy thinking is not mystery.

**Acknowledge Complexity:**
Sometimes the answer is "both/and" or "it depends." That's okay. Show your reasoning.

**Trust the Magisterium:**
When Church teaching is clear, present it clearly. When there's room for faithful disagreement or theological development, acknowledge that. We're orthodox Catholics, but orthodoxy doesn't mean incurious or afraid.

### Literary Analysis Standards

**Close Reading:**
Engage with specific scenes, dialogue, and details from the text. Don't just summarize plot.

**Character Analysis:**
Explore motivations, moral choices, character development. Ask:
- What virtues or vices does this character embody?
- How do their choices reveal truth about human nature?
- What would Catholic moral theology say about their decisions?

**Authorial Intent (When Known):**
- Tolkien: Explicitly Catholic, anti-allegory but pro-applicability
- Lewis: Anglican but deeply compatible with Catholic thought
- Greene: Tortured Catholic exploring faith in extremis
- Heinlein: Libertarian, provocative, not religious
- Others: Research as needed

**Don't Force It:**
Not every detail needs a Catholic interpretation. Sometimes a cigar is just a cigar. Our task is to find genuine resonance with Catholic truth, not to baptize everything.

**Typology and Prefigurement:**
Following the model from 2025's *Dune* essay, you can explore how Old Testament prefigures New Testament, or how these stories prefigure Christian truth. But be careful:
- Make clear when you're drawing parallels vs. claiming authorial intent
- Avoid shallow "Christ figure" analysis (lazy literary criticism)
- Look for deeper structural and thematic resonance

### Contemporary Application

**Make It Matter:**
Connect the book's themes to contemporary life. Readers should finish your essay seeing their own lives differently.

**Avoid Cheap Culture War Points:**
We're not writing political screeds. If you reference contemporary issues:
- Stay focused on perennial moral questions, not partisan talking points
- Show how the book illuminates the issue, not how the issue proves your politics
- Be charitable to those who disagree

**Examples from 2025:**
- *Ender's Game*: Government overreach and religious persecution
- *Pride and Prejudice*: Modern misunderstandings of marriage
- *The Martian*: Attitude toward work and suffering

## V. Discussion Question Crafting

### Purpose of Discussion Questions
These questions are **the engine of our monthly meetings**. They need to generate rich, substantive conversation that:
- Draws out different perspectives
- Connects the book to lived experience
- Explores theological depth
- Invites personal reflection without being therapy
- Can sustain 20-30 minutes of discussion each

### The Five-Question Structure

**Question 1: Textual/Character Focus**
Ground the discussion in the book itself. Focus on a key character, scene, or decision.

**Good Examples:**
- "What do you think of Ender's experiences with his siblings? How do you think they shaped and affected each other in terms of identity?"
- "What do you think of Darcy's interference between Bingley and Jane? Do you think his actions were warranted as a good friend?"

**Question 2: Theological/Moral Analysis**
Dig into the Catholic/moral dimension of a theme from the book.

**Good Examples:**
- "Do you think the religion of Speaker for the Dead is a Mormon understanding of the need for the Sacrament of Confession?"
- "What do you think of Ender's position that if he has to fight, he must win so definitively that there are no more fights afterwards, aka the doctrine of overwhelming force? What are the moral arguments for and against this position?"

**Question 3: Personal/Relational Application**
Invite readers to consider how the book's themes touch their own lives.

**Good Examples:**
- "Which character stories did you find the most compelling and relatable? Why?"
- "What is our role in getting our own family members and friends to heaven? What do we owe to each other in this life as Christians?"

**Question 4: Broader Implications**
Zoom out to cultural, philosophical, or contemporary issues.

**Good Examples:**
- "What do you think of what Battle School and Command School taught Ender about leadership? Are there valid morals and virtues that they taught the kids?"
- "Do you think there are parallels to when Jesus told the Apostles (and us, today) that he is coming back, or that he has already overcome the world? How does knowing the end of the Christian story help us sustain our hope in this life?"

**Question 5: Open-Ended/Provocative**
End with a question that could go in multiple directions, ensuring the conversation doesn't feel "finished."

**Good Examples:**
- "Do you think Lewis imagined hell as a place with no community or relational qualities, but heaven as a place where only relationships can help us move upwards?"
- "Do you think the upswing of suicide (including medically assisted suicide) is as much due to the loss of humor as the loss of hope? Why or why not?"

### Question-Crafting Principles

**Open-Ended:**
Avoid yes/no questions unless you follow with "Why or why not?" Most questions should begin with:
- "What do you think of..."
- "How do you think..."
- "Why do you think..."
- "Do you think... Why or why not?"

**Not Leading:**
Don't telegraph the "right" answer. Trust your readers to think.

**Bad:** "Don't you think Frodo's burden represents the weight of sin we all carry?"
**Good:** "What do you think Frodo's burden represents? How does it change him?"

**Mix Analytical and Personal:**
Balance questions about the text with questions about application. Not every question needs to be deeply personal, but at least 2 of 5 should invite personal reflection.

**Contemporary Relevance:**
At least 1-2 questions should connect to contemporary life, culture, or moral questions we face today.

**Avoid:**
- Questions that just recap plot ("What happened when...")
- Questions too broad to answer ("What is the meaning of life?")
- Questions that require specialized knowledge ("What does Thomistic metaphysics say about...")
- Therapy questions ("How does this trigger your childhood trauma?")

### Testing Your Questions
Before finalizing, ask yourself:
1. Could this question sustain 20 minutes of conversation?
2. Would different people have different valid answers?
3. Does it connect to the book in a meaningful way?
4. Does it invite depth without requiring a theology degree?
5. Would I personally want to discuss this question?

## VI. Technical Specifications

### Markdown Formatting

**Headings:**
```markdown
# Title (H1 - only for the essay title)
## Subheadings (H2 - for major sections)
### Sub-subheadings (H3 - use sparingly)
```

**Emphasis:**
- *Italics* for book titles: `*The Power and the Glory*`
- **Bold** for emphasis: `**This is critical**`
- Use bold sparingly—impact diminishes with overuse

**Block Quotes:**
```markdown
> "This is a quote from the book or a source."
> — Author Name, *Book Title*
```

**Lists:**
- Use numbered lists for discussion questions
- Use bulleted lists for examples or supporting points
- Keep list items parallel in structure

**Horizontal Rules:**
Use `---` to create section breaks (after title/author, before discussion questions)

### Length Expectations

**Target: 10-20 pages (3,000-6,000 words)**

This is a guideline, not a straitjacket. Some books will demand more space, others less. Quality over arbitrary word count.

**Too Short (Under 2,500 words):**
Likely means you haven't done deep enough analysis. Keep digging.

**Too Long (Over 7,000 words):**
Consider whether you're:
- Repeating yourself
- Going down tangents
- Trying to cover too much
- Needing to split into focused subsections

**Check Against 2025:**
- *Ender's Game*: ~5,000 words (excellent depth)
- *Pride and Prejudice*: ~6,500 words (justified by complexity)
- *The Great Divorce*: ~3,500 words (appropriate for shorter book)

### Citation Style

**Catechism:**
`*Catechism of the Catholic Church* (para. 1234)`

**Scripture:**
`— John 15:13` or `(John 15:13)`
Use book name, chapter:verse format
Major translations (RSV-CE, NABRE, Douay-Rheims) are all acceptable

**Saints and Church Fathers:**
`— St. Augustine, *The City of God*`
`— St. Thomas Aquinas, *Summa Theologica*, I-II, Q. 94, Art. 2`

**Encyclicals and Church Documents:**
`— Pope John Paul II, *Evangelium Vitae* (para. 12)`

**The Books We're Analyzing:**
You don't need formal citations for the books being studied. Readers have them. Reference by:
- Character name
- Scene description
- Chapter (if helpful)
- Direct quotes when powerful

**Other Sources:**
If you reference historical facts, theological concepts, or other books, make it clear in context. Footnotes are optional but can be useful for deeper dives.

### Cross-Referencing

**Within 2026:**
Feel free to reference other books on the 2026 list, especially:
- The LOTR trilogy (across March, July, November)
- Thematic connections (e.g., authority in *Starship Troopers* vs. *Master and Commander*)

**Format:**
"As we explored in *The Fellowship of the Ring*..."
"This theme will return when we read *Hyperion*..."

**Back to 2025:**
You can reference 2025 books if genuinely relevant:
"Like Ender Wiggin, this character faces..."

**Rule: Must Stand Alone**
Every essay should be complete on its own. Cross-references should enhance, not be required for comprehension.

### File Naming and Organization

**File Names:**
Use lowercase with hyphens:
- `the-power-and-the-glory.md`
- `starship-troopers.md`
- `the-fellowship-of-the-ring.md`

**Directory Structure:**
```
docs/
  study-guides/

      introduction.md
      the-power-and-the-glory.md
      starship-troopers.md
      [etc.]
      acknowledgements.md
```

**Introduction and Acknowledgements:**
Like 2025, we'll have:
- **Introduction**: Sets the "War is Heck" framework, introduces the year's arc, explains methodology
- **Acknowledgements**: Brief, personal, humble

## VII. Practical Workflow

### Before You Write

1. **Read the book** (obviously, but read it with the theme in mind)
2. **Take notes** on:
   - Key scenes that illuminate virtue/vice in conflict
   - Characters who face moral choices under pressure
   - Themes that connect to Catholic teaching
   - Questions the book raises
3. **Research as needed**:
   - Author's background and intent
   - Historical context
   - Relevant Church teaching
4. **Identify your central thesis**: What is the ONE big idea this essay explores?

### Drafting

1. **Choose your opening quote**: Should illuminate your thesis
2. **Write a strong hook**: First paragraph should grab attention
3. **Follow your outline** (if you made one) but stay flexible
4. **Write discussion questions early**: They can clarify your thinking
5. **Don't self-edit while drafting**: Get ideas down, polish later

### Revision

1. **Check structure**: Does the argument flow logically?
2. **Tighten prose**: Cut unnecessary words, strengthen weak passages
3. **Verify citations**: Are quotes accurate? Sources properly attributed?
4. **Test discussion questions**: Read them aloud. Would you want to discuss them?
5. **Read the whole thing aloud**: Catches awkward phrasing, pacing issues
6. **Check against this style guide**: Hit all required elements?

### Collaboration (Tyler & Sadie)

Since you're splitting the essays:
- **Coordinate** on which books each of you will write
- **Read each other's drafts**: Not to rewrite, but to ensure consistency
- **Share notes**: You might catch things the other missed
- **Respect each other's voice**: Don't try to make everything sound the same
